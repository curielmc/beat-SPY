# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Students (high-school / college age) learning investing by competing. They join a class via a teacher, build a virtual fund from real-market data, and race a leaderboard to beat the S&P 500. Secondary roles: teachers running classes (monitoring students, fund analytics, assigning training) and admins. The primary surface — and the design priority — is the student game loop: pick stocks, track performance vs SPY, climb the leaderboard, learn from training.

## Product Purpose

An educational stock-trading simulator. Students manage virtual portfolios with real tickers, see attribution and performance against the S&P 500 benchmark, enter competitions, and follow a social feed — all inside a teacher-managed classroom. Success = students stay engaged (return daily, trade, compete) AND actually learn investing concepts. The benchmark framing ("beat SPY") is the core motivational hook.

## Brand Personality

Playful and competitive, with real credibility. Three words: motivating, energetic, trustworthy. The interface should feel like a game worth winning — leaderboards, streaks, wins celebrated — while still reading as a legitimate finance tool a student can take seriously. Voice: encouraging coach, not a stuffy broker. Wins feel good; losses are framed as learning, never punitive.

## Anti-references

- **Bloomberg / trading-terminal density** — no walls of tiny numbers, no intimidating data overload. This is for learners.
- **Childish / cartoonish edtech** — despite being a game, avoid kiddie mascots, primary-color clutter, or anything that undercuts the finance credibility.
- Generic SaaS-template look (Inter-on-purple-gradient, card-in-card).

## Design Principles

- **Make the benchmark visceral.** Every performance view should make "am I beating SPY?" instantly readable — the core hook deserves the strongest visual treatment.
- **Game feel, finance trust.** Reward and celebrate progress (leaderboard, streaks, wins) without sacrificing the credibility of a real market tool.
- **Lower the intimidation floor.** A first-time student should never feel lost in jargon or dense tables; progressive disclosure over data dumps.
- **Learning is built in, not bolted on.** Training, attribution, and feedback live alongside the game loop, not in a separate boring corner.
- **Clarity wins over cleverage.** When density and legibility conflict, favor legibility — the user is here to learn, not to day-trade.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA. Gain/loss and benchmark cues must not rely on red/green color alone — pair with sign, arrows, or labels for color-blind users. Respect reduced-motion for the celebratory animations. Sufficient contrast on the dark DaisyUI theme.
